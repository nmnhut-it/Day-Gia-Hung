May, Might, Could - Possibility

Structure: S + may/might/could + base verb

He might win!
She could become a famous artist.
It may rain tomorrow.

Note: No difference in meaning between may, might, and could for possibility

Negative Form

Structure: S + may/might/could + not + base verb

It might not rain.
They may not come to the party.
He could not win this year.

With First Conditional

Use for possibility (not certainty) in conditional sentences:

If you practice, you could win. (possible, not certain)
If you practice, you will win. (certain)

Structure:

  • Present: have to / has to + base verb
  • Past: had to + base verb
  • Negative: don't/doesn't/didn't have to
  • Question: Do/Does/Did + have to?
I have to study hard.
She has to wear a uniform.
He had to practice yesterday.
I don't have to play every day. (not necessary)

‚ö†Ô∏è Important Rules

  • Modal verbs (may/might/could):
    - No -s in third person (He might, NOT mights)
    - Always + base verb (may go, NOT may goes)
    - No 'to' (could win, NOT could to win)
  • Have to:
    - Third person: has to (NOT have to)
    - After do/does/did: have to (NOT has to)
    - don't have to = not necessary (NOT forbidden)
